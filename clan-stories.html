Story № 1 · The Chief

Why I am bringing back the clan

Fergus Commane, The Commane — Chief of Clan Ó Comáin · Newhall House, County Clare

People ask me why now. Why, after eight centuries of silence, the clan should rise again in 2026. The honest answer is that it began with my son.

Ronan Ó Comáin was [age] years old when he died on [date]. [One or two sentences in Fergus's own voice about who Ronan was — what he loved, what made him who he was. Specifics matter here. Music? Books? The land at Newhall? The kind of detail that lets a reader feel they have met him in a single line.]

[Fergus, in your own words: what happened in you after Ronan was gone. The decision to do this work. Was it immediate or did it come slowly? Was it grief, was it duty, was it something he had asked of you, was it something you chose for him? The honest sentence here will carry the whole piece.]

[A single sentence from above, lifted out — the most important line Fergus writes. Often the line he didn't expect to write.]

Clan Ó Comáin has existed since the seventh century. It is recorded in the annals; it is in the ground at Cahercommane; it is in the bones of those who carry the name today across Ireland and the diaspora. What was lost was not the clan itself but the gathering of it — the act of saying, together, we are still here. That is the work. [Fergus may want to make this his own; the paragraph reads as me speaking, not him.]

So when a member joins now, in this first year of the revival, they are joining something that began in a particular grief and has become something larger than it. [Fergus: a sentence here about what you want members to feel. Welcome? Witness? Kinship?] Whatever they bring — a Cummins surname misspelled across three continents, a great-grandmother who left Clare in 1880, a single Y-DNA match they didn't know what to do with — they bring it home.

The clan is rising in his name. Caithfidh an stair a bheith i réim. History must prevail.
