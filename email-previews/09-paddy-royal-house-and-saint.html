Ó COMÁIN

The Seanchaí's year-end letter

The royal house and the saint

Dear Aoife,

Paddy here. The year is nearly out, and I owe you the longer telling. Pour yourself something. Settle in. This is a Seanchaí's letter, the way they were always meant to be told — not from a textbook, but as a story passed down to a kinsman who has just come home.

We start on the Burren, on the high stone ridge, four thousand years back.

The people called themselves the Déisi Muman — the Déisi of Munster — and they were a confederation of fierce, learned, wandering peoples whose original seat was in what is now County Waterford. They pressed westward, generation after generation, until their leading line reached the limestone country of Clare and built a fort there. Cahercommane. Three concentric stone walls, raised by the bare hands of men whose names are long gone and whose memory the limestone has kept for us.

Time moved on, as time does. The fort flourished as the seat of the chiefdom through the early medieval centuries, when our line stood among the kings of the Déisi. And here's where the story takes a turn that I love most in all of it.

In the seventh century, a man named Commán was born of the line. He chose the cloister over the chieftaincy. He went out into the wild west of Ireland and founded the monastery at Roscommon — Ros Comáin, the wood of Commán — and another at Kinvara on the bay. He healed people. He preached the Gospel to the chieftains who still drank their wine in the old halls. He died in odour of sanctity, and the Church recognised him as a saint, and he is venerated to this day.

Saint Commán is our patron. He is who the clan calls upon. The shamrock border around our coat of arms is for the protection of the Holy Trinity, the saint, and the deep ecclesiastical strand in the clan's heritage. The harp the mermaid plays is for the music of the line. The mermaid herself is for Newhall Lake — the household's mystical guardian, in the story we tell about her.

From the saint, the line carries through the medieval centuries. The Lords of Kinvara, who held court at the bay. The Comyns of Corcomroe and Kilcorney, formally registered with the Ulster King of Arms in 1748. The line ran on, anglicised as Coman, Commane, Comyn, Cummins — but the line itself unbroken in name and place.

Then came the long erasure. The Penal years. The Famine. The Catholic Gaelic clans were dismantled — the lands taken, the chiefs scattered, the names anglicised by clerks who could not hear what their bearers were saying. Ó Comáin became Cummins, Commins, Commons, Comyn, Coman, Hurley — the same line wearing different names, the way a river runs the same water through a hundred miles of country. The diaspora carried those scattered names to America, Australia, the cities of Britain. The clan was scattered across the centuries, but the line was always there.

And here we come to the part that is happening now.

What is occurring is a revival. The Chief's consecration under Brehon law. The recognition by Clans of Ireland. The rebuilding of Newhall as the household seat. The gatherings, the Register, the kindred — none of this is invention. It is restoration. The line was always there. What was missing was the household, and that is what is being rebuilt — with your name, now, among those rebuilding it.

And there is one more thread, the hardest to tell. The revival in our own day was not begun for legal reasons or for heritage reasons alone. It began in 2023, in a particular grief, in the Chief's own family. Maria has told that story in her own words. I will not retell it here. But you should know that the work you are now part of began there — and the love that has gathered the kindred since is the answer the family chose to give it.

That, in the shape of a Seanchaí's telling, is the story of the line you carry. You are not joining a club. You are taking your place in a real royal house, with a saint at its centre, with a four-thousand-year line behind it, in active modern revival.

I wanted you to hear it told, before the year was out. Sleep on it. Carry it.
