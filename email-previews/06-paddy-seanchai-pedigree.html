Ó COMÁIN

A word from the Seanchaí

The royal house you have joined

Dear Aoife,

Paddy here — Paddy Commane of Ballymacooda. I serve the clan as Seanchaí (SHAN-a-kee), the keeper of the stories.

A few months in now, and I wanted to put down clearly a thing that ought to be said: the line you've joined is a royal one, and an old one.

The line runs from the Bronze Age — four thousand years and more — through the kings of Déisi Muman in the south of Ireland, up into the Burren of County Clare where our ancestors built Cahercommane. The fort still stands, ringed in stone walls you can walk today.

From there, through the long medieval centuries, the name shifted under outside pressure — Ó Comáin became Comyn, and Comyn became Cummins, Commons, Hurley, and the rest. But the line did not break.

There's a saying among us Seanchaithe — names are like rivers. They run through different country and pick up new colours, but it's the same water all the way down. The river of Ó Comáin runs through your name too, whatever shape it takes today.

The full account is on our pedigree page — forty-five footnotes, drawn from the Annals, the Brehon Genealogies, the archaeology at Cahercommane, and Y-DNA analysis.

If you only read one thing about the clan, read this. It will change what you think about your name.
