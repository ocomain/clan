The Order of Honours

Three honours are conferred by the Chief upon members who bring others to the Register at Newhall. Each is a raising in rank within Clan Ó Comáin — recorded under the Chief's seal, held quietly with the Herald, and carried by the member as a mark of what they have built. The honours follow one another: the higher is taken up; the lower is laid by.

Cara
KAR-uh
Friend
Conferred at one

The first of the honours, conferred when a member welcomes their first soul into the Register at Newhall. Cara is the older Irish word for a friend — and in Brehon-law tradition, the Chief's cairde were the trusted kin-allies who carried his interests outward into the wider world. The dignity of Cara names exactly that role: a member through whom the clan reaches another.

To bring even one to the clan is no small thing — it is the act on which all kinship is built.

By the Chief's command, the Privy Council, the Office of the Private Secretary, and the kindred of Ó Comáin, both within and without the boundaries of the clan's historic seat at Newhall, do acknowledge and address a Cara henceforth as:

For example
James Comyn, Cara of Ó Comáin
Ardchara
ARD-khar-uh
Friend of high standing
Conferred at five

The second of the honours, a raising in rank from Cara. Ardchara joins the prefix ard- (high, lofty) to cara (friend) — so the title reads, by its parts, as Friend of high standing. Where Cara names the friend, Ardchara names the high friend — the clan's recognition that the bearer carries the work of welcome with particular grace.

To bring five is the work of a member who has made the clan their own. The Chief is the better for knowing it.

By the Chief's express command, the Privy Council, the Office of the Private Secretary, and the kindred of Ó Comáin, both within and without the boundaries of the clan's historic seat at Newhall, shall hereafter acknowledge and address an Ardchara, in the dignity now conferred, as:

For example
James Comyn, Ardchara of Ó Comáin
Onóir
UH-nor
One held in honour
Conferred at fifteen

The highest of the honours conferred within Clan Ó Comáin. Onóir carries the weight of its meaning: where Cara names the friend and Ardchara names the high friend, Onóir names the dignity itself — a member whose contribution has been marked, and who is now held by the Chief and the Herald as one of the clan's standing-bearers. It is conferred upon a member who has brought fifteen souls to the Register at Newhall, and the language of the warrant is the formal chivalric register: "It hath pleased the Chief to raise you within the clan to the dignity of Onóir."

By this raising the member henceforth bears the title Onóir, with the place and standing belonging to that rank in the clan's keeping. There are very few in any generation who carry fifteen to the Register, and the Chief takes a particular interest in such members.

From this raising, the bearer stands in the kindred's keeping among those most honoured by Clan Ó Comáin — the members through whom the work of welcome most prevails.

By the Chief's sovereign command, the Privy Council, the Office of the Private Secretary, and all the kindred of Ó Comáin, both within and without the boundaries of the clan's historic seat at Newhall, do acknowledge and address an Onóir henceforth, with the place and standing belonging to that rank, as:

For example
James Comyn, Onóir of Ó Comáin
On women and gender-neutral courtesy titles

In the older Gaelic tradition, women held a respected standing uncommon in mediaeval Europe — owning property in their own right, retaining their lineage in marriage, and at times — as with Granuaile of the O'Malleys — taking up the leadership of the clan in their own hand. The honours of Cara, Ardchara and Onóir are accordingly conferred without distinction of gender: the dignity is granted to whichever member — man, woman, or otherwise — has earned it by their welcome to the clan. The honour itself attaches to that named primary grantee alone, and is recorded under their name. By the kindred's courtesy, however, the spouse of the bearer (whether wife, husband or partner) may be addressed by the same dignity in social usage — as in the older European convention by which Lady is addressed alongside Sir, the household's standing reflecting the bearer's own.

— Clan Herald at Newhall
