Ancient origins

4,000 years in Ireland

Big-Y DNA confirms the male line in the Bronze Age population that settled western Ireland. The clan's haplogroup signature places it within the shared Atlantic Celtic ancestry of Ireland, Scotland, Wales and Brittany — and connects, at a deep prehistoric level, to the line from which Brian Boru's Dál Cais dynasty also descends.

Chapter II

The royal pedigree

c. 357 — 1225 AD
Kings of Uí Maine · the Connacht royal line
Máine Mór Cairbre Crom Breanan Dall Coman mac Breanan Dall

The O'Hart pedigree, drawn from the Books of Lecan and Leinster, traces the clan's royal descent through the kings of Uí Maine. Breanan Dall (d. 597/601 AD) is the earliest individually named direct ancestor in a primary historical source; his son Coman is the name-giver from whom "Ó Comáin" — descendant of Coman — derives.

Chapter III

Kings of Déisi Muman

7th — 8th c.
The Munster royal line and the coming to Clare
Indarba na nDéisi Suibne mac Comáin Congal mac Suibne Eóganachta alliance

The Expulsion of the Déisi — one of the oldest surviving Old Irish prose texts — records the kindred's flight from Leinster and their settlement in Munster under alliance with the Eóganachta. This is the political geography in which Suibne mac Comáin's kingship, and the Munster side of the Ó Comáin line, unfolded.

Chapter V

The Chiefdom of Tulach Commáin

8th — 13th c.
An ancient Gaelic chieftain line in the Burren
Uí Chuinn Kingdom of Cashel Gibson 1990 CUP 2012

Identified by Gibson (UCLA 1990; Cambridge University Press 2012) as the ruling lineage of the chiefdom, the Uí Chuinn — the medieval form of the name — held Cahercommane and large parts of the Burren in the 8th and 9th centuries within the wider political landscape of the Kingdom of Cashel.

Chapter VI

Cahercommane

c. 800 AD
The ancestral capital — archaeology and place-name
Tri-vallate stone fort Hencken 1934 Cotter 1999 Burren plateau

The tri-vallate stone fort on the edge of the Burren plateau — excavated by Hencken in 1934, reassessed by Cotter in 1999 — is the inauguration site and ancestral capital of the chiefdom. Its very name, Cathair Chomáin, preserves the Comáin identity in stone.

Chapter VII

The fall of a chieftain line

1225 — 1585
Dispossession and the documentary gap
Conall O'Comain, 1225 Anglo-Norman dispossession Inauguration site

Conall O'Comain, last recorded head of the clan in the O'Hart pedigree, marks the end of territorial authority under the Anglo-Norman dispossession of 1225. The clan's formal inauguration site at Tulach Commáin confirms this was no ordinary noble family — it was a genuine Gaelic chieftain line whose fall closed an ancient Clare order.
