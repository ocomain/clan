Chapter I

Ancient origins

4,000 years in Ireland

The Ó Comáin family's presence in Ireland is not measured in centuries but in millennia. Big-Y DNA testing confirms the direct male line of the family carries haplogroup R-L21 — the defining genetic marker of the Celtic peoples of Ireland, Scotland, Wales and Brittany — placing their male ancestry within the Bronze Age population that settled the western edge of Ireland over 4,000 years ago.1

The family's specific haplogroup, R-L1066, has been identified in ancient remains from Scotland dating to 1500–1300 BC, with a further ancient individual carrying a closely related branch identified between 43 BC and 117 AD — reflecting the shared ancestry of the Atlantic Celtic world at a time when Ireland and Scotland were effectively one people.2

The terminal haplogroup R-FT100195 — a 12-tester block within R-BY14247 — represents the family's specific medieval-era branch. The closest living Big-Y matches include Cornelius Moylan (Ó Maoláin, a Clare-Munster surname concentrated in north Clare and east Galway), J. II Burke and Joseph Walsh — sitting in an Ireland-dominant cluster representing the most promising living genealogical leads for connecting the modern Ó Comáin family to its medieval Clare ancestors across the documentary gap of the 13th to 17th centuries.3

The result sits on the R-Z2534 branch — a deep Bronze Age population marker carried in Ireland by multiple separate Munster kindreds. The Ó Comáin male line splits from the Z2534 substratum at a level upstream of any of the named medieval Irish dynasties, with a common ancestor estimated at approximately 1500 BC — long before the kindreds and lordships of historical Clare took shape. The line is firmly Munster-Clare in modern surname distribution, and definitively non-Dalcassian on direct testing.4

Chapter II

The royal pedigree

Clann Comáin — the Ó Comáin line in the Uí Maine pedigree

The Uí Maine pedigree of the Ó Comáin line is preserved in the Book of Lecan (Royal Irish Academy MS 23 P 2, fol. 90–92), the great fifteenth-century manuscript compilation of Irish genealogy, where the kindred is given its own named segment — Clann Comáin — within the Uí Maine tract. The pedigree was first published in modern critical edition by John O'Donovan as The Tribes and Customs of Hy-Many, commonly called O'Kelly's Country (Dublin: Irish Archaeological Society, 1843), and was subsequently carried into John O'Hart's Irish Pedigrees (1892). It traces the family's documented descent through the kings of Uí Maine in Connacht.5

In the Book of Lecan, the medieval Irish genealogists gave the kindred its own named segment within the great Uí Maine pedigree tract — Clann Comáin, the descendants of Coman.

Book of Lecan, RIA MS 23 P 2, fol. 90–92, c. 1418

The named segment is itself evidence against the older derivation of the surname from cam ("bent/crooked"). The medieval pedigree tracts organised themselves around named eponymic ancestors — Clann X meaning "the descendants of" X — not around physical traits. The descent here is traced through ten generations back to a specific man called Coman, son of Brenainn Dall, twelfth king of Uí Maine. Edward MacLysaght, the first Chief Herald of Ireland, expressly set aside the cam derivation in Irish Families; the manuscript form supports him.

The Kingdom of Uí Maine was established by Máine Mór mac Eochaidh around 357 AD, centred on what is now east Galway and south Roscommon. It became one of the most important provincial kingdoms of medieval Connacht, producing a remarkable number of the great Gaelic dynasties of the west of Ireland.6

Cairbre Crom, 11th King of Uí Maine (fl. c. 556 AD), is the critical ancestral figure from whom almost all subsequent Uí Maine dynasties descend. Through Cairbre Crom, the Ó Comáin family shares common ancestry with the Clancy, Kelly, Madden, Tracy, Hannan, Kenny, Colman, Egan and Larkin families — many of the great Gaelic dynasties of Clare and Connacht.7

Breanan Dall, 12th King of Uí Maine, whose death is recorded in the Annals of Ulster at 597 or 601 AD, is the earliest individually named direct ancestor of the Ó Comáin family documented in a primary historical source.8 He is the father of Coman, from whom the surname Ó Comáin directly derives.

Coman mac Breanan Dall (fl. 620–650 AD) is traditionally identified as the name-giver of the family — the original Coman from whom "Ó Comáin," descendant of Coman, derives. Whether the later Déisi Muman kings Suibne mac Comáin (d. 658 AD) and his son Congal mac Suibne (d. 701 AD) descend from this same Coman directly — through a cousin-line sharing the Uí Maine ancestry — or from a separately-named Comán within the Déisi Muman dynasty itself, remains part of the open scholarly question presented in the next chapter.

The last recorded head of the family in the manuscript is Conall O'Comáin — terminal generation of the Clann Comáin segment in the Book of Lecan. The pedigree is not carried forward beyond Conall — consistent with the family's loss of territorial headship in the early thirteenth century, traditionally dated to the 1225 grant of the Lordship of Connacht to the De Burc family by Henry III. After this point the family lost territorial authority, but the name continued through the subsequent centuries.5a

Chapter III

Kings of Déisi Muman

The Munster royal line and the coming to Clare

Alongside the Connacht royal pedigree, the medieval Irish annals record a parallel tradition of the Comáin name in Munster — specifically in the Déisi Muman (the Déisi of Munster), a distinct kingdom within the province. The Déisi Muman were not the provincial monarchs of Munster — that title was held by overlord dynasties such as the Eóganachta and, later, the Dál gCais — but a people with their own kings, their own territory, and a political standing substantial enough to be recorded repeatedly in the primary sources.

The very origin of the Déisi Muman is itself preserved in medieval Irish literature. The Old Irish narrative The Expulsion of the Déisi (Indarba na nDéisi) — one of the earliest surviving Old Irish prose texts — records that the kindred was forced out of the Tara region of Leinster, following a dispute during the reign of the High King Cormac mac Airt, and eventually settled in Munster, where they formed an alliance with the Eóganachta to secure their new territory. This is the political geography within which the kingship of Suibne mac Comáin was later exercised, and the historical background from which the Ó Comáin name descends on its Munster side.

Annals of Ulster, 701 AD

The Cáin Adamnáin — enacted in 697 AD and regarded as the first written law in Western history to protect women, children and non-combatants from violence in war — carries Congal's name (son of Suibne) among the surviving list of royal guarantors, an independent attestation of the family's standing alongside the High King of Ireland and other lesser kings.

The guarantor list of the Cáin Adamnáin names four concurrent kings of the Déisi — Congal mac Suibne; Andelaith, king of the Déisi Tuaiscirt, the Northern Déisi whose territory lay in what is now County Clare and whose descendants became the Dál Cais; Eochaid mac Dúnchada, king of the Déisi; and Elodach, king of the Déisi. Four simultaneous kings bearing the same royal title is not a contradiction but a signature of how Déisi kingship worked. It was not a single line. It was a federation of septs, each politically significant, each electing kings in rotation.13b

Suibne mac Comáin, who died in 658 AD, is recorded in the annals in the succession of kings of Déisi Muman — one of the earliest historical figures bearing the Comáin name in any primary source.9 His son Congal mac Suibne — the Irish patronymic mac meaning "son of" — who died in 701 AD, is also named in the Annals of Ulster as king of the Déisi Muman.10

Pender's Déssi Genealogies preserves dozens of named Déisi septs, each a recognised kin-group with its own territorial identity. Kingship rotated among them — which is why Suibne's own son Congal did not succeed him directly. The kingship passed first to other septs before returning to the Commán-descended line at Congal's accession, a span of more than thirty years from father to son.12

The annals record the deaths of two further figures bearing the patronymic mac Commáin: Célechair mac Commáin, killed at the battle of Corcu Modruad in 704 or 705 AD — fought in the kingdom of the Corcu Mruad in north-west Clare, a neighbouring kingdom to the Chiefdom of Tulach Commáin and its capital at Cahercommane (Annals of Innisfallen, Annals of Ulster, Annals of the Four Masters), and Colmán mac Commáin, who died on the Aran Islands in 751 AD (Annals of Inisfallen) — celebrated in the martyrology Félire Óengusso as a bishop of Munster and described in Leabhar Breac as one of the four sages of Ireland.11

Their exact lineage remains an open scholarly question. Gibson (1990) proposed, on the basis of later medieval genealogical tracts, that they may have been of the Eóganacht Uí Cormaic — but Gibson himself expressly conceded that "a coherent case cannot really be made at this juncture with the scattered indications" (Gibson 1990, p. 394). Cotter (1999) echoes the same caution, noting the brother-relationship between Célechair and Colmán "could have been" established but is not proven. Pender's Déssi Genealogies complicates the picture further: it places the Uí Cormaic among the Déisi Muman septs themselves — "cenél úa Cormaic lasna Déssib muman", Rawlinson B 502 and Book of Lecan — suggesting this kindred sat on the boundary between the Eóganacht and the Déisi rather than belonging cleanly to either.12

Gibson's 1990 framing did not engage with the two named Déisi Muman kings above — Suibne mac Comáin (d. 658) and his son Congal mac Suibne (d. 701). In their presence, it is at least as plausible that Célechair and Colmán belonged to the Déisi Muman lineage carrying the Comáin name across the 7th and 8th centuries. The broader Eóganacht–Déisi Muman relationship — overlord and subject kingdom within Munster — does not require us to choose between them: both traditions place the family firmly within Munster's royal architecture of the 7th and 8th centuries.

This carries a direct implication for the chiefdom site itself. Pender's evidence is that the Déisi Muman were not a single line but a federation of dozens of named septs, each with its own recognised territory and each electing kings in rotation. On that model, the Chiefdom of Tulach Commáin — adjacent to Corcu Mruad, with its capital at Cahercommane, "Commán's stone fort" — is most readily explained as the territorial seat of a Déisi-Muman sept of the Commán line: the kindred to which Suibne and Congal belonged, and within whose ambit Célechair and Colmán plausibly stood. The annalistic record of named Commán-line kings in the Déisi tradition, the Pender attestation of the Uí Cormaic among the Déisi septs, the documented Déisi political reach into north-west Clare by 765 AD, and the place-name evidence of Cahercommane and Tulach Commáin all point in the same direction: a Déisi-Muman sept holding its own chiefdom on the Burren frontier, its caput a stone fort bearing the eponymous ancestor's name.13c

The annalistic evidence from the Déisi Genealogies, written in the Book of Lecan, Rawlinson B 502, and the Book of Leinster, establishes the family's presence across both the Connacht and Munster traditions from the earliest recorded period.12 Whether these represent one family or two independent lines sharing the personal name Comáin remains an open scholarly question — both traditions are presented here honestly.

Could name-giver Coman of Uí Maine be the father of King Suibne of the Déisi Muman?

The precise relationship between these two figures — Coman mac Breanan Dall and Suibne mac Comáin — has not been directly addressed by published scholarship. The primary sources are silent on any link between them. The chronology is, however, such that a connection cannot be ruled out: Coman's floruit of c. 620–650 AD places him in the generation immediately preceding Suibne's kingship (d. 658 AD), and the Déisi king's patronymic — literally "son of Comán" — would, on the common reading, identify simply an unrelated Comán within the Déisi lineage itself.

That said, early medieval Gaelic Ireland saw kingship move between dynasties through maternal lineage, political alliance, and displacement far more fluidly than the later idealised king-lists suggest. Royal blood from one house could legitimise a man's claim in another, particularly where the mother's kin were themselves royal. The 630s saw Congal Cáech of the Cruithní claim the kingship of Tara itself — an outsider from a different province entirely — and the annals record many further examples of cross-dynastic rule that the tidy genealogical tracts would not have predicted.

Whether Suibne of the Déisi was the son of the Uí Maine name-giver — bridging the two traditions in a single generation — or the son of a separate Comán within the Déisi lineage itself cannot be resolved from the surviving evidence. It is a question the annals leave open for future scholarship, and one that the geography — Uí Maine descent in the north, Déisi kingship in the south, and the Ó Comáin chiefdom settled in north Clare between them — at least invites.

Archaeologist Claire Cotter suggests that the Burren area may have been named after a sub-king named Commán, noting evidence in the annals — including the Annals of Ulster recording the 744 AD destruction of Corcu Mruad by the Déisi — consistent with Gibson's analysis of the chiefdom. The 744 entry is direct: "Foirddbe Corcu mudruadh don Deiss" — the overthrow of the Corcu Mruad by the Déisi.13

By the generation after Congal, Déisi rule had extended into north-west Clare. The annals record the death in 765 AD of Torpad mac Cernaig, styled tigerna na nDéisi — lord of the Déisi — in the Annals of the Four Masters, and rí Corcu Mruad — king of the Corcu Mruad — in the Annals of Inisfallen. The Corcu Mruad were the people of north-west Clare — a neighbouring kingdom to the Chiefdom of Tulach Commáin and its capital at Cahercommaun. Two independent annal traditions, the same man, the same year, two titles across two peoples. Cahercommaun's occupation belongs to precisely this period of Déisi presence in the wider Burren — the material record and the annals meeting on the same ground. Torpad's dual title is the clearest primary-source illustration of how early Irish rule crossed dynastic boundaries — by conquest, marriage, or political alliance. The Déisi had overthrown the Corcu Mruad in 744; twenty years later, one man held lordship of one and kingship of the other. It is the clearest documented instance in the Commán-name tradition of the political fluidity that sits underneath every question on this page.13a

Chapter IV

Saints and scholars

The ecclesiastical heritage of the Comáin name

Saint Commán founded Roscommon — Ros Commáin, meaning "Commán's Wood" — and the ancient church at Kinvara in County Galway, on the southern edge of the Burren. His death is recorded in the Annals of Ulster at 747 AD.14 The Dictionary of National Biography (1887) is explicit that these are one and the same person — the entry states that Saint Commán of Roscommon "also founded the church of Ceann Mara, now Kinvara" — placing both foundations with a single saint whose ministry extended from Roscommon to the shores of the Burren.15

Kinvara sits directly on the southern shore of the Burren — the very heartland of the ancient Chiefdom of Tulach Commáin — placing the saint's foundation within sight of the ancestral capital Cahercommane. Roscommon town and county bear the saint's name to this day, making Saint Commán one of the most geographically enduring of all Irish saints.

Colmán mac Comáin, whose death on the Aran Islands is recorded in the Annals of Inisfallen at 751 AD, is called one of the four sages of Ireland — a remarkable designation placing a member of the Comáin family among the great learned men of early medieval Ireland.16

Gilla Cómáin mac Gilla Samthainde (fl. c. 1072 AD) was a celebrated medieval Irish poet whose works were incorporated into the Lebor Gabála Érenn — the Book of Invasions — one of the great manuscripts of Irish literature.17

The family name is also preserved in Ferchess mac Commán — a fian warrior and poet in early Irish mythology, a member of the household of King Ailill who ruled southern Ireland, recorded in the ancient sagas as having avenged the death of Ailill's son.18

Chapter V

The Chiefdom of Tulach Commáin

An ancient Gaelic chieftain line in the Burren

The Chiefdom of Tulach Commáin — "The Mound of Commán" — was the principal political entity of the Ó Comáin sept in early medieval Clare. The chiefdom occupied territory in the Burren, the extraordinary limestone plateau of north Clare, and its capital was the great triple ring stone fort of Cahercommane.19

According to the Cambridge University Press analysis of the chiefdom by D. Blair Gibson, Tulach Commáin was an important but not dominant chiefdom within the broader political landscape of the Kingdom of Cashel, controlling or heavily influencing large parts of the Burren in the 8th and 9th centuries.20

The 1585 deed of Queen Elizabeth I references "Tullagh Coman" as a place name — confirming the persistence of the Comáin name in the Burren landscape into the Elizabethan period, centuries after the chiefdom had faded from political life.21 Thomas Johnson Westropp, the Irish antiquarian and president of the Royal Society of Antiquaries of Ireland, documented the site in his early 20th century surveys, noting that the personal name Commán was likely the root of both the fort and the townland names.22

As late as the 19th century, Tulach Commáin — the burial and inauguration site of the chieftains — was regarded as a supernaturally potent spot by local tradition. A local told an archaeologist: "it has more fairies than all the other forts of the hill."23

Scholarly recognition: the 1990 UCLA doctoral dissertation

In 1990, the American anthropologist Dr. David Blair Gibson submitted his doctoral dissertation to the Department of Anthropology, University of California, Los Angeles. His committee chair was Prof. Timothy Earle — one of the foremost authorities on chiefdom archaeology. The dissertation's title alone is significant: Tulach Commáin: A View of an Irish Chiefdom.GS

Gibson reconstructed, on archaeological and territorial grounds, a north-Clare polity centred on the stone fort of Cahercommaun (Cathair Commáin — "the Dwelling of Commán"). Its companion prehistoric mound, Tulach Commáin ("Mound of Commán"), stands a short distance away and gives its name to the townland of Tullycommon, still shown on Ordnance Survey maps. Both the settlement and the mound bear Commán's name. Gibson identified the mound as the probable inauguration cairn of the chiefdom's chiefs — the place where each new chief was invested with office, in the ancient Gaelic kingship tradition.

Gibson reconstructed Cahercommaun as the political centre of an early medieval chiefdom whose name and territorial extent are preserved in the local placenames Cathair Chommáin and Tulach Commáin. His work was later expanded into the Cambridge University Press monograph From Chiefdom to State in Early Ireland (2012). The 1990 dissertation remains the foundational scholarly treatment of the chiefdom — and it carries Commán's name in its very title.

Chapter VI

Cahercommane

The ancestral capital — archaeology and place name

Cahercommane — Cathair Chomáin, "Fort of Commán" — is a triple ring stone fort on the south-east edge of the Burren, in Kilnaboy, near the village of Carran in County Clare. The inner wall alone used an estimated 16,500 tons of stone, the outer wall measuring approximately 350 feet east-west by 245 feet north-south. The fort sits dramatically on the edge of an inland cliff, with three concentric walls reaching to the cliff edge.24

The site was excavated in 1934 by the Third Harvard Archaeological Expedition, led by Hugh O'Neill Hencken, in a six-week excavation that established the fort as the capital of an early medieval chiefdom. Archaeological evidence found of settlement dating back to the 5th and 6th centuries, with the fort itself built in the 9th century. A silver brooch found in one of the souterrains — similar in design to the Tara brooch and now held in the National Museum of Ireland — confirms occupation by the 9th century AD.25

In his 1938 report, Hencken retained the spelling Cahercommaun despite the Ordnance Survey map showing Cahircommane — "in spite of possible linguistic objections" (Hencken 1938, 3). Cotter (2012, p.83) confirms Westropp used both "Cahircommane or Cahercommaun." Both agreed the name derived from the personal name Commán. Cotter establishes that the valley containing Cahercommane, Cashlaun Gar and Glencurraun "suggests strongly that the second element of the fort's name at least includes the personal name Commán," and that the element -aun derives from Irish -án, not -an(n).26

Cotter (1999, in Cotter 2012) considered which of the contending dynasties of 7th–8th century north Clare might have given the fort its name, listing the principal contenders as the Corcu Modruad, Uí Fidgeinti, In Déis Tuaiscirt, the Connachta and older populations. She suggested — in carefully hedged terms — that the site "might have gained its name from an Uí Fidgeinti sub-king, Commán, or to a lesser degree from a Ciarraige sub-king, Cumann," following Gibson's earlier note that Célechair mac Commáin's genealogy preserved in the medieval corpus placed him in the Uí Chormaic, taken there as a branch of the Uí Fidgeinti.26

A further dynastic alternative was not addressed in Cotter's contender list: the Déisi Muman. The Annals of Ulster entry that Cotter herself records — "Foirddbe Corcu mudruadh don Deiss", the destruction of the Corcu Mruad by the Déisi in 744 AD — places a Déisi-aligned kindred on the north-Clare frontier in precisely the period of Cahercommane's high occupation. Pender's Déssi Genealogies (1937) explicitly places the Uí Cormaic among the Déisi Muman septs themselves — "cenél úa Cormaic lasna Déssib muman" (Rawlinson B 502, 143b; Book of Lecan) — meaning the same Uí Chormaic kin-group that Gibson tentatively assigned to the Uí Fidgeinti is, in another body of medieval Irish manuscript evidence, a Déisi Muman sept. Read alongside the named Déisi Muman kings of the Commán line — Suibne mac Comáin (d. 658, Annals of Inisfallen) and his son Congal mac Suibne (d. 701, Annals of Ulster; guarantor of the Cáin Adamnáin in 697) — the Déisi Muman attribution emerges as the more economical reading of the primary sources. The chiefdom of Tulach Commáin reads as the territorial seat of an Ó Comáin sept of the Déisi Muman, holding Cahercommane as a frontier outpost against Corcu Mruad to the north-west. See Chapter III for the fuller primary-source argument and footnote 13c for the four-strand evidentiary stack.

Cotter (2012, p.90) also identifies the 1585 deed in The Irish Fiants of Elizabeth I — referencing "Dermod O'Flanygan of Cahirekamon" and "Tullagh Coman" twice — as the earliest documentary mention of both names. Crucially, on the geography of the chiefdom, Gibson's 1990 reconstruction map of Tulach Commáin (Figure 11.1, p. 344) places Cahercommane firmly within the Tulach Commáin chiefdom, with Corcu Mruad to the north-west, Uí Fiachrach Aidne to the north, Uí Flánchadha to the east and Uí Dedaid to the south. Cahercommane was on the Corcu Mruad frontier — never within Corcu Mruad territory.GS

There are no signs of occupation past the 10th century — the chiefdom appears to have dissolved or been absorbed into the expanding Dál Cais confederacy under the O'Brien dynasty. The site was abandoned, but the name survived, embedded in the landscape in both the place name Cahercommane and the nearby townland of Tullycommon (Tulach Commáin).27

Chapter VII

The fall of a chieftain line

Dispossession and the documentary gap

By the 13th century, the Ó Comáin family — chiefs of the ancient Chiefdom of Tulach Commáin, with their own inauguration site at Cahercommane — were dispossessed of their ancestral lands following the Anglo-Norman invasion, when King Henry III granted the Lordship of Connacht to the De Burc (Burke) family in 1225.28

The last recorded head of the family, Conall O'Comáin, is named at this point in the Clann Comáin segment of the Book of Lecan, after which the manuscript line is not carried forward — consistent with the family losing its territorial authority and patrimony after centuries of chiefly rule. That the clan possessed a formal inauguration site at Tulach Commáin, and that the medieval genealogists recognised Clann Comáin as a distinct segment within the Uí Maine tract, confirms this was no ordinary noble family but a genuine Gaelic chieftain line whose dispossession marked the end of an ancient order in Clare.29

The documentary gap between 1225 and the first post-medieval census record of 1659 — four and a half centuries — reflects the broader destruction of Gaelic Irish institutional memory during the years of Norman and later Tudor conquest. The family survived, but their history survived only in fragments: a place name, a pedigree in the great manuscripts, scattered annalistic references.

The gap may be bridged in future through the Registry of Deeds (from 1708), the Conyngham estate papers, the Civil Survey of 1654, the Hearth Money Rolls of 1664, and the Fiants of the Tudor Sovereigns — all sources that may yet yield documentary evidence of the family in the intervening centuries.

Chapter VIII

The name endures

From Ó Comáin to Comyn — the family across the medieval gap

The apparent silence of the Clare landholding records on the name "Ó Comáin" between the 1225 dispossession and the 1659 census is not a silence about the family. It is a silence about the spelling. Across the late medieval and early modern centuries — under Anglo-Norman administration, Tudor reconquest, and English-language record-keeping — the family is not absent from the documentary record. It is recorded under different anglicised forms, the principal one being Comyn.

Two factors drive this. First, after the 1225 loss of territorial headship and the rise of the Dál Cais (the dynasty of Brian Boru, the O'Briens of Thomond, the MacNamaras and the O'Deas) as the dominant political power in Clare, the Ó Comáin sept was a non-Dalcassian Gaelic kindred living within Dalcassian-ruled Clare. Surname assimilation toward an officially-recognised, Anglo-Norman-acceptable form was a documented strategy of survival for Gaelic families who had lost their own territorial standing. Second, in the absence of any standardised Irish-language orthography until An Caighdeán Oifigiúil (the official Irish-language standard) was published in 1948, the family name was written in a plethora of phonetic anglicisations by English-speaking administrators recording the name by ear: Coman, Coeman, Comen, Comane, Commane, Cuimín, Cumin, Cummins, and — most often, in Clare — Comyn. The modern standardised form "Ó Comáin" is, in this sense, a 20th-century convention applied retrospectively to a name that for seven centuries existed in many spellings.

The Comyn-in-Clare pattern is documentary, not speculative. A family of Comyn is recorded in Limerick by c. 1440, with Sir William Comyn, knight, documented as ancestor of the Comyns of Corcomroe and Kilcorney — the Kilcorney family being seated in the Barony of Corcomroe in the Burren, the very heartland of the ancestral chiefdom. A pedigree of this family was registered with the Ulster King of Arms in 1748.30

An Elizabethan deed of 1585 references "Tullagh Coman" as the name of a townland — preserving the ancient Chiefdom of Tulach Commáin in the legal record of the English administration four centuries after the dispossession.31

In 1619, James Comyn received Doonbeg Castle from Daniel O'Brien — evidence that a family bearing the Comyn/Commane name retained sufficient standing in Clare to receive a castle grant from the O'Brien lordship, centuries after the formal loss of their own territory.32

Around 1650, the Ulster King of Arms manuscript records a coat of arms under the heading "Coman" / "Comen" on a folio devoted to Clare armigerous families — placed alongside the arms of the O'Dea and other recognised Clare Gaelic-noble families. Coman is the standard 17th-century anglicisation of Ó Comáin; Comen appears in the same period in the secretary hand where the letters "a" and "e" are notoriously close, and where heraldic visitation books commonly record the same family under multiple spellings on different folios. The 1650 entry on a Clare-families page constitutes recognition of the name as an armigerous Clare lineage of the period — at the very moment when, on Luke McInerney's reading, the family is undocumented in landholding records. The two facts are reconciled by the Comyn-anglicisation pattern: the family is in the Clare records of the 17th century — under "Coman," "Comen," and "Comyn," not under "Ó Comáin." For the fuller treatment of the 1650 manuscript and the heraldic question, see the coat of arms page.

Pender's Census of 1659 records "Comane &c" — 11 persons — in the Barony of Tulla, County Clare: the first confirmed post-medieval population record placing the family in Clare under that spelling, following the centuries-long Comyn-era anglicisation.33

Edward MacLysaght, the first Chief Herald of Ireland and author of Irish Families (1985), is the modern scholarly authority for the Comyn-as-Gaelic reading. He confirmed that the name appears as Ó Comáin in Munster — "whence come the majority of present day Cumminses (also called Commane) now found in Counties Tipperary and Cork" — and explicitly treats the Clare Comyns as Gaelic, or at least not necessarily Norman, despite many published Irish Comyn pedigrees focusing on Scots-Norman origins which now seem incorrect.34 The name Na Comáin is confirmed by Logainm.ie as a townland in the Iveragh barony of Kerry, in the Gaeltacht area of Dromod parish, confirming the family's geographic distribution across the Munster seaboard.35

The picture is not entirely simple. Some Clare Comyn families did have genuine Norman descent — through the small Anglo-Norman Coimín line that entered Munster as Uí Briain mercenaries (the line later represented by the 18th-century Gaelic poet Mícheál Coimín of Miltown Malbay). Other families appear to have acquired Norman pedigrees during the 17th-century heraldic visitations, when documented Gaelic ancestry had often been lost and a Norman descent was the readier route to a recognised arms grant. Y-DNA testing of any specific Clare Comyn family is now the most reliable means of distinguishing genuine Norman descent from Gaelic-anglicised lines.

Big-Y testing of the Ó Comáin male line (Chapter X) corroborates MacLysaght's Gaelic-anglicised reading directly. The match cluster of the Ó Comáin patriline includes the Hiberno-Norman surnames Burke and Walsh sitting on a deep Irish Y-line that diverged from these surname-bearing populations approximately 1,500 years before the Norman arrival in Ireland. This is the documented genetic pattern of Gaelic Irish families adopting Norman-era surnames after the 12th century — and the same biological pattern applies to Comyn in Clare. The Y-DNA evidence is what MacLysaght could not have had in 1985: direct confirmation that the deep Irish patriline carries surnames that are typologically Norman, exactly as expected if Gaelic kindreds adopted Norman-era surnames in the centuries following the conquest.

The heraldic record of the period adds further documentary weight. A shield inscribed Coman (in another reading Comen) appears on page 83 of an Ulster King of Arms manuscript dated to approximately 1650, on a page recording the arms of Clare nobility — placed alongside the arms of the O'Dea family, an established Clare Gaelic line. The entry records armigerous standing for the name in mid-17th-century Clare, alongside other recognised noble families of the county. Whether the entry represents the Gaelic Ó Comáin/Coman line or the Anglo-Norman Comyn family is debatable on the manuscript alone — the secretary hand of the period frequently confused a and e — but with the Y-DNA evidence above, the older default reading that the entry must represent the Norman Comyn family becomes less compelling. The entry is at minimum direct documentary evidence that the Coman/Comen name held armigerous recognition in Clare a decade before the family appears in Pender's Census of 1659 as "Comane &c" in the Barony of Tulla.33a

The Big-Y DNA evidence corroborates MacLysaght's Comyn-as-Gaelic reading from a wholly independent direction: the Ó Comáin patriline sits in a deep Irish Y-line cluster that includes Norman-surnamed families (Burke, Walsh) on the same line — exactly the documented pattern of Gaelic Irish kindreds who, after the 12th century, adopted Norman-era surnames for legal, economic or social reasons. See Chapter X for the full Y-DNA findings.

Chapter IX

The modern record

From the Tithe Books to the present

Among the individuals identified in the documented modern Commane line is John Commane, of Newhall, recorded as a tenant in Ballyea townland, Killone parish, County Clare in the Tithe Applotment Book of 1828 — Ballyea and Killone lying in the same immediate territory as Newhall, marking a subtle but resonant historic connection to the clan's present seat. His neighbour in the same townland was Mich'l Hehir — a remarkable echo across more than a millennium of the two ancient Clare families of the Burren sharing territory.36

John Commane is absent from Griffith's Valuation of 1855 for Killone parish. A common scholarly reading is that he either died during the Great Famine of 1845–1852 or emigrated in the Famine period — as so many Clare families did. A Commane Road in Baldwinsville, New York (Onondaga County) testifies to Famine-era emigration of the family to upstate New York, where the family left a road name as their legacy in the American landscape.37

It is also worth noting, however, that absence from a 19th-century government survey is not in itself proof of death or emigration. A third possibility, preserved in the family's own oral tradition, is that some Commane households were simply not recorded. Mistrust of British administrative surveys ran deep among the Gaelic tenantry of Clare — born of fear of eviction, rent increases, conscription, and the long memory of the Penal Laws — and it was not uncommon for heads of household to quietly instruct their families not to give their names when officials came to the door. Stories of this practice survive in the living Commane line to the present day. Continuity in place therefore remains a possibility alongside the death and emigration readings of the 1855 record.

The Clare Comyns — erenaghs, poets and French nobility

MacLysaght's Irish Families (1972) records a branch of the Clare Comyn family of particular distinction. Michael Comyn (1688–1760), of Kilcorcoran, County Clare — a man of the same Clare sept — was one of the most celebrated Gaelic poets of the 18th century, composing in the classical Irish tradition at a time when the Gaelic literary order had been largely destroyed by the Penal Laws.41

His son, Michael Comyn (b. 1704), followed the path of the Wild Geese — the Irish Gaelic soldiers, scholars and families who fled into exile on the Continent after the Jacobite defeat of 1691. He emigrated to France, where he was accepted as one of the nobility of France — a formal recognition of his Gaelic aristocratic lineage by the French court, which maintained a register of Irish noble families in exile throughout the 18th century.42

A Clare Comyn — of the same sept as the Ó Comáin of Cahercommane and Newhall — was accepted as a member of the French nobility. His grandson died on the scaffold in Paris in 1793.

His grandson, John Francis Comyn (1742–1793), was guillotined as an aristocrat during the French Revolution — a Clare family that had risen from the stone forts of the Burren to the aristocracy of France, and was destroyed in the Terror that swept that aristocracy away. The arc of this single line — from a 7th-century Gaelic kingship to a 1793 Parisian scaffold — encapsulates the full trajectory of the Gaelic world in three centuries of exile.43

David Comyn (1853–1907), another Clareman of the name, was active in the cultural movements of the late 19th century that led to the formation of the Gaelic League — the organisation that would become the principal vehicle of the Irish language revival and a direct precursor to the independence movement. The Clare Comyn tradition of letters, from the 18th-century poet to the Gaelic League activist, represents a continuous thread of Gaelic cultural commitment across two centuries.44

MacLysaght also records that the family were historically erenaghs — hereditary custodians of church lands — of the church of St. Cuimín Fada in Connacht, with the parish of Kilcuimin on the western shore of the Bay of Killala named after the saint. This ecclesiastical custodianship, which placed the family among the hereditary religious aristocracy of Connacht, is consistent with the family's documented connection to Saint Commán — founder of Roscommon — recorded across multiple primary sources.45

John O'Donovan's Tribes and Customs of Hy-Many (1843), the Irish Archaeological Society's critical edition of the Uí Maine tract from the Book of Lecan, was the first published source to make the Clann Comáin pedigree available to modern scholarship. John O'Hart's Irish Pedigrees (1892), drawing on the same manuscript tradition, subsequently compiled the descent into a popular Anglicised form that brought the family's documented genealogy to a wider readership.38

The Harvard Archaeological Expedition's excavation of Cahercommane in 1934, and D. Blair Gibson's Cambridge University Press monograph From Chiefdom to State in Early Ireland (2012), provided the most detailed modern scholarly analyses of the chiefdom — establishing Cahercommane as a major early medieval political centre and situating the Ó Comáin family within the broader political geography of early medieval Clare.39

Chapter X

DNA — 4,000 years confirmed

The genetic record of the Ó Comáin male line

Big-Y DNA testing of the Ó Comáin male line confirms the family's ancient Irish roots through haplogroup R-L21 — the defining genetic marker of the Celtic peoples of Ireland, Scotland, Wales and Brittany — placing their male ancestry within the Bronze Age population that settled the western edge of Ireland over 4,000 years ago.1

The complete confirmed haplogroup chain is: R-M269 → R-L23 → R-L51 → R-P310 → R-L151 → R-P312 → R-Z290 → R-L21 → R-S552 → R-DF13 → R-ZZ10_1 → R-Z253 → R-Z2534 → R-ZZ5_1 → R-Z2185 → R-BY44331 → R-Z2186 → R-Z2183 → R-L1066 → R-FTT21 → R-FGC35529 → R-BY14247 → R-FT100195 (terminal). FT100195 is a 12-tester block within R-BY14247 representing the family's specific medieval-era branch.

R-L21 is the defining haplogroup of the Irish and Celtic peoples — Ireland dominates the distribution with 678 matches.2 R-Z253 is strongly Irish, with Ireland having 110 matches against Scotland's 47. R-Z2534 is a deep Bronze Age branch carried by multiple Munster kindreds; the Ó Comáin line diverges from the Z2534 substratum upstream of any of the historical medieval dynasties of the region, with the split estimated at approximately 1500 BC. The line tests definitively non-Dalcassian (L226 negative) — distinguishing it from the lineage of Brian Boru and the O'Brien, MacNamara and O'Dea families of Clare, whose patrilines descend through the L226 marker.4

Two branches of the same Bronze Age substrate

The Z2534 split has historical resonance. The Dál gCais — the dynasty of Brian Boru — descend through Z2534 → L226 → Z17669: the Déisi Tuisceart (the "Northern Déisi"), who pushed north into Clare and rebranded under the new dynastic name Dál gCais in the early 10th century. The Ó Comáin male line descends through Z2534 → Z2185 → L1066 → FT100195: the same Z2534 substratum, but a different downstream branch. Standard scholarship now accepts the Dál gCais as descended from the Déisi Tuisceart; the Y-DNA evidence places the Ó Comáin patriline on the parallel southern branch — the Déisi Muman — of the same biological substrate. Same deep Bronze Age ancestor c. 1500 BC; divergent medieval trajectories.

The FT100195 cluster — closest living matches

R-L1066 has been identified in ancient remains from Scotland dating to 1500–1300 BC, with a further ancient individual dated between 43 BC and 117 AD — placing the deeper branch in the documented genetic landscape of Bronze Age and Iron Age Atlantic Britain.2 Within the terminal FT100195 block the closest living Big-Y matches include Cornelius Moylan (Ó Maoláin — a Clare-Munster surname concentrated in north Clare and east Galway), J. II Burke (Ireland), and Joseph Walsh (Ireland). At one step upstream (R-FGC35524) sits Robert Harry McGregor of Scottish Highland descent (Aulich, c. 1650) — likely a Dál Riata-era split. The cluster geography is overwhelmingly Clare-Munster, with the older Highland Scottish branch consistent with the documented Irish-to-Scottish migrations of the early medieval period.3

Two of the Big-Y matches in the FT100195 block carry Anglo-Norman surnames — Burke and Walsh — yet sit on a deep Irish Y-line that diverged from these surname-bearing populations approximately 1,500 years before the Norman arrival in Ireland. This is the documented genetic pattern of Gaelic-Irish families adopting Norman-era surnames after the 12th century, while their actual male-line descent remained Gaelic. The same pattern applies symmetrically to the Comyn name in Clare: Edward MacLysaght's documentary position that Comyn in Clare is the anglicised form of an Irish surname (Irish Families, 1985) now has direct genetic confirmation from the match cluster of the Ó Comáin male line itself. The Comyn-as-Gaelic reading developed in Chapter VIII does not rest on documentary inference alone — the Y-DNA carries it.

What the DNA rules out — and what it supports

The Big-Y data rules out three previously-considered ancestries with specificity. Uí Maine (Connacht): the cluster contains no Kellys, Maddens, Naughtons, Fahys, Donnellans, or Concannons — surnames that should appear in dense numbers if the line had been continuously biological Uí Maine for 1,500 years. The Uí Maine connection on this page is therefore best read as the surviving record of the family's medieval pedigree heritage — preserved in the Book of Lecan as Clann Comáin within the Uí Maine tract — rather than as the line of unbroken male-line descent. Uí Fidgeinti (Limerick): no O'Donovans, MacEnerys, McSheehys, or O'Collins — running against Cotter's (1999) attribution of Cahercommane to a Uí Fidgeinti sub-king Commán. Dál gCais: definitively ruled out by the L226-negative result. The Ó Comáin male line is none of these.

What the evidence supports is descent from one of the pre-Dalcassian peoples of Clare and west Munster — the older kindreds that the Dál gCais consolidation in the 9th–10th centuries absorbed, eclipsed, or pushed sideways. The Ó Comáin patriline shares the deep Z2534 ancestry with the Dál gCais through a parallel Déisi-derived branch, sits within a Clare-Munster surname cluster, and is consistent with descent from a Déisi Muman frontier sept that pushed into Clare in the 8th century and held Cahercommane against the Corcu Mruad — the historical scenario the documentary evidence in Chapters III and VI supports. The DNA does not on its own prove Déisi Muman descent: the FT100195 founder predates any of the named medieval kindreds by several centuries. What it does is converge with the documentary record on a single coherent reading — a Munster-Clare line of pre-Dalcassian origin, present in the region for at least 1,500 years before any of the surnames recognisable today.

Chapter XI

How the evidence converges

A Déisi Muman frontier sept holding Cahercommane against Corcu Mruad

This page presents the case for the early-medieval origin of the Ó Comáin family across ten chapters. What emerges, when the strands are read together, is a single coherent reading.

Four converging strands

Pedigree. The medieval Irish genealogists preserved the family as Clann Comáin — a named segment within the Uí Maine pedigree tract of the Book of Lecan (RIA MS 23 P 2, fol. 90–92, c. 1418), traced through ten generations from Conall O'Comáin (terminal generation, line ends with the 1225 dispossession) back to the eponymic ancestor Coman, son of Brenainn Dall, twelfth king of Uí Maine, and ultimately to Maine Mór, founder of Uí Maine c. 357 AD. The pedigree's terminal point at Conall, while the principal Uí Maine lines continue forward in the same manuscript, is itself consistent with the family's loss of territorial headship in the early thirteenth century. (Chapter II.)

Royal annals. The annals of Ireland record named kings of the Déisi Muman bearing the Comáin name across the 7th and 8th centuries: Suibne mac Comáin (d. 658, Annals of Inisfallen), his son Congal mac Suibne (d. 701, Annals of Ulster, Annals of Tigernach, Chronicon Scotorum, Annals of Roscrea; named guarantor of the Cáin Adamnáin in 697), and the patronymic mac Commáin in Célechair (d. 705, killed at the battle of Corcu Modruad in north-west Clare) and Colmán (d. 751, on the Aran Islands; one of the four sages of Ireland in Leabhar Breac). (Chapter III.)

Frontier annals. The 744 Annals of Ulster entry — "Foirddbe Corcu mudruadh don Deiss", the Déisi destruction of the Corcu Mruad — places a Déisi-aligned kindred on the north-Clare frontier in precisely the period of Cahercommane's high occupation. The 765 dual title of Torpad mac Cernaig — lord of the Déisi (Annals of the Four Masters) and king of the Corcu Mruad (Annals of Inisfallen) — places a single Déisi-aligned ruler over both kingdoms in the immediate Cahercommane-occupation period, documenting the political reach of the Déisi Muman across north-west Clare. (Chapters III, VI.)

Fort archaeology and geography. The chiefdom of Tulach Commáin — reconstructed in Gibson's 1990 dissertation (Figure 11.1, p. 344) and 2012 Cambridge monograph — held Cahercommane on the eastern edge of the Burren, with Corcu Mruad as a neighbouring kingdom to the north-west. Cahercommane was on the Corcu Mruad frontier, not within it. The fort itself, a triple ring stone fort whose inner wall used 16,500 tons of stone, was occupied through the 7th to 9th centuries and abandoned by the 10th — and it took its name from a personal name Commán (Cotter 1999, 2012; Hencken 1938; Westropp), a kindred-leader rather than a physical descriptor. (Chapters V, VI.)

Y-DNA. Big-Y testing of the modern Ó Comáin male line places the patriline at terminal SNP R-FT100195, on the Z2534 branch parallel to but distinct from the Dál gCais (L226-negative). The match cluster is overwhelmingly Clare-Munster, with no Uí Maine surname signal, no Uí Fidgeinti surname signal, and the Anglo-Norman surnames Burke and Walsh sitting on the deep Irish Y-line — direct genetic confirmation of MacLysaght's Comyn-as-Gaelic position. The line is consistent with descent from a pre-Dalcassian Munster kindred that pushed into Clare in the 8th century. (Chapter X.)

The reading

Read together, the strands converge on a single coherent reading: the chiefdom of Tulach Commáin was held by an Ó Comáin sept of the Déisi Muman — a frontier kindred holding Cahercommane against the Corcu Mruad to the north-west — within the wider political ambit of Cashel as the overlord dynasty of Munster, and within the federation of named Déisi Muman septs (preserved in Pender's standard edition of the Déssi Genealogies) that elected kings in rotation across the 7th and 8th centuries.

The kindred's medieval pedigree heritage is preserved in the Uí Maine tract of the Book of Lecan, where they appear as a recognised segment — Clann Comáin — with their own descent traced back to Coman of Uí Maine. The relationship between the Déisi Muman political identity in the south and the Uí Maine pedigree-affiliation in the north cannot be resolved from the surviving evidence alone — early-medieval Gaelic kingship moved between dynasties through maternal lineage, political alliance, and displacement far more fluidly than the later idealised king-lists suggest. What can be said is that the documentary record places the family within both traditions, that the genetic record places the male line on the Munster side, and that the chiefdom of Tulach Commáin sits geographically and chronologically at the Burren frontier where the two strands meet.

What remains open

Two questions remain genuinely open. The exact dynastic affiliation of the Cahercommane fort-name — whether the founder Commán was a Déisi Muman sub-king, a member of the Uí Cormaic kindred that Pender places within the Déisi Muman septs, or (per Cotter 1999) a Uí Fidgeinti sub-king — cannot be resolved without further primary-source work. This page presents the Déisi Muman reading as the better-supported alternative without dismissing Cotter's published Uí Fidgeinti attribution; both readings remain in the scholarly record. And the relationship between Coman of Uí Maine and Suibne of the Déisi Muman — whether they were kinsmen, father and son through cross-dynastic marriage, or unrelated figures sharing the same Comáin name-tradition — cannot be resolved from the surviving manuscripts. These are the questions where future research will continue.

The convergence of the four strands above, however, is sufficient to establish the early-medieval origin of the Ó Comáin family within the political and biological landscape of Munster, and the Burren chiefdom of Tulach Commáin as the territorial seat of an Ó Comáin sept holding Cahercommane against Corcu Mruad — the case this page has set out to make.

Chapter XII

The clan revival

2025 — a new chapter in an ancient story

Clan Ó Comáin was officially recognised by Clans of Ireland — Finte na hÉireann — in 2025, under the patronage of Michael D. Higgins, President of Ireland. The committee of Clans of Ireland verified the antiquity of the Irish Gaelic name and the clan's rightful place among the historic families of Ireland.40

The clan revival was inspired by the memory of Ronan James Patrick Commane (1989–2023), the second son of Fergus Commane, Chief of Clan Ó Comáin. Ronan's love for his extended family and for all people inspired the rebirth of the clan in his memory.

Fergus Commane, as Taoiseach successor consecrated by derbhfine under Brehon law, leads the clan from the ancestral seat of Newhall House in County Clare — within the same Clare landscape where the family's name has been recorded continuously since the 7th century. He is also custodian of Killone Abbey and the Holy Well of St John the Baptist on the Newhall Estate.

The clan has also been noted in Burke's Peerage and is listed in the Clans of Ireland Register 2026. Antoin Commane serves as Clan Tanist, representing the clan at official gatherings including the 2026 Clans of Ireland assembly in Dublin.

The clan welcomes all who carry the name in any of its variants, all who trace their roots to Ireland, and all who love Gaelic culture and heritage — from County Clare to Chicago, from Kerry to New York, from Munster to Melbourne. The story of Clan Ó Comáin is one of the oldest in Ireland — and it is far from over.
