Commins - O Comain - Connacht and Clare

The Commins family -- a Connacht branch of an ancient Clare lineage

Commins is the Connacht form of O Comain. The name connects to one of the oldest and most thoroughly documented Gaelic clans in Ireland.

Where does the Commins name come from?

The surname Commins is the anglicised form of the ancient Irish Gaelic name O Comain, particularly prevalent in Connacht -- as opposed to Cummins, which is the more common Munster form. In the Griffiths Valuation of 1847-1864, 150 families were recorded as Commins across Ireland. The name is documented in Edward MacLysaght's The Surnames of Ireland as a variant of O Comain, sharing the same ancient Gaelic root as Cummins, Commons and Commane.

The Commins name carries the same meaning as all its variants: descendant of Coman -- the personal name of a 7th-century ancestor of the clan. Whether the name has survived in Ireland, emigrated to America, Australia or England, it connects the bearer to one of the oldest documented Gaelic lineages in County Clare.

The Commins name -- rare enough to have survived largely intact -- is among the most direct phonetic preservations of O Comain. If you carry it, you carry one of the ancient clan's most faithful anglicisations.

A lineage 1,400 years in the making

The clan's documented history begins in 658 AD with Suibne mac Comain, king of the Deis Munster -- recorded in the Annals of Ulster. Through the Book of Lecan and Book of Leinster, the lineage extends to the royal line of Ui Maine in Connacht -- fitting for a name whose Connacht variant is Commins specifically.

Clan O Comain was officially recognised by Clans of Ireland in 2025. The clan's ancestral capital, Cahercommane in the Burren, and its clan seat at Newhall Estate in County Clare, are open to all registered members for the annual summer festivals and gatherings.
